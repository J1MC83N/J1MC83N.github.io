    Furnace @ Home

    DSC04933.jpg

    Zinc is a great metal.

    It's hefty, naturally shiny, very skin-friendly, reasonably hard, and has a melting point of 419.5˚C, below the Curie point of iron. Why is last point important? Because it means you can build a zinc-melting furnace out of any induction cooker. 

    A run-down of the science: an induction cooker heats up ferromagnetic metals like iron and nickel; it does so with a coil that, when powered with fast alternating current, creates current in said metals via electromagnetic induction which heats them up via the via the skin effect. The catch is that ferromagnetism stops working above the Curie point, which for iron is 770˚C, and so induction cookers stop working at that point as well. Thus theoretically speaking, your induction cooker can heat your iron/steel pan all the way up to red hot, but no further. Sure, a good propane burner can almost melt the same pan with a white hot flame, but personally I find induction science cooler. 

    DSC04635.jpg

    So the idea was this: take the cheapest induction cooker online, rewind the heating coil around an iron crucible as well as some generous amount of thermal insulation, and we got ourselves a homemade furnace that can melt zinc with all renewable energy, no open flame required. Simple idea really that turned out pretty fruitful; I'm surprised no one on the internet has tried something similar. 

    18-yo me was super proud of himself for coming up with that idea and was already envisioning his glorious zinc-casting career. Because, a fun fact about me, I've always liked metallic objects, just something about the heft and durability of them that tickled some obscure part of my brain. And now you're telling me that, instead of collecting drawers full of scrap metal parts, I could be making shiny metal objects all I want at home? Yes please. 

    Now the keen-eyed metal-philic reader may wonder, why not melt and cast aluminum? It melts at 660˚C which is also below 770˚C, and it's a much more popular choice for metal casting hobbyists and for good reasons. The short answer is that 660˚C is a bit too hot for my home-grown furnace to reach and sustain, also latent heat of fusion sucks. The smarter-sounding answer is that science doth not make a working furnace, engineering does, and I'm far from an engineer (no software engineering doesn't count).

    The main engineering challenge of this "induction furnace" [1] is essentially 1) crucible needs to be hot, 2) coil needs to cool, and 3) these two things need to be physically close to each other for the whole thing to work. Point 1 is straightforward, the crucible should be at and preferably above the melting point of whatever metal it holds. Point 2 stems from the fact that induction cooker coils are made from enameled copper wires (specifically Litz wires), and the enamel layer breaks down at as little as 105˚C. Point 3 is because magnetic field generated by the coil drops off fast with distance to the whatever it is heating up. 

    To tackle these challenges, I designed my furnace as a layered bowl, hot things inside, cool things outside. From inside to outside, the layers are:

    • Iron crucible;

    • Thermal insulation: at 419.5˚C ceramic fiber is really the only option (but also a huge pain to work with);

    • Structure material (i.e. bowl): to hold the bowl shape;

    • Coil wires;

    • Thermal conductor: to suck heat away from wires and also hold/stick wires to the bowl;

    • Water cooling tubes;

    • High temperature protection for the tubes: in case I accidentally spill molten zinc (yes it has happened and I've learned my lessons). 

    [1] why the quotation marks? Because the term induction furnace is already taken and means something slightly different. Yes our furnace also melts metals with induction, but an actual induction furnace uses much higher-frequency AC to heat any conductive material to much higher temperatures without relying on the skin effect. Our furnace could technically be called a low-frequency induction furnace, but I think that title is undeserved.
